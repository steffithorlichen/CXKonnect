Culture Exercise

The Culture Contract

Each person commits to one behavior that makes the org function as one team. Group finds themes and writes shared commitments.

60 min
Any team
8–15 people
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🤝
Purpose
Build Shared Commitments
Move from abstract values to specific, observable behaviors — commitments each person makes to the team and the team makes to every customer.
👥
Who Should Attend
Any CX Team
Works across a single function or cross-functionally. Most powerful when all functions that share customer accountability are in the room together.
📄
Output
A Signed Culture Contract
3–5 shared commitments, grounded in individual behaviors named by the team, signed by every person in the room. Not a values poster — a behavioral agreement.
✍️

Core Objective

Culture is not what you say you value — it is what behavior you reward, tolerate, and model. This exercise makes culture explicit by translating it into specific commitments each person is willing to make in public, to their colleagues, about how they will behave when it is difficult. The contract is not a document. It is a shared agreement about what this team looks like when it is at its best — and a standing invitation to call each other back to it when it isn't.

1
Facilitation Guide
60 min total
P1
5
min
Set the Frame — What a Commitment Is
Facilitator-led

Open by distinguishing between values and commitments. A value is a belief. A commitment is a behavior — something another person could observe you doing or not doing. "We value collaboration" is a value. "When I have information a colleague needs to serve a customer better, I share it before they ask" is a commitment. The exercise asks only for commitments.

  • Explain the format: each person will write one commitment — the one behavior they believe, if everyone practiced it, would most change this team's ability to function as one unit.
  • The commitment must be stated in first person ("I will…") and must be specific enough that someone else could tell you whether you kept it.
  • Clarify that the commitment is about working together as a team — not about serving customers directly. The customer benefit is downstream. The commitment is about how we operate with each other.
Facilitator Note

The most common failure mode in this exercise is commitments that are too generic to hold anyone accountable to. "I will be more communicative" is not a commitment — "When I learn something about a customer that changes another function's risk picture, I will communicate it within 24 hours" is. Coach for specificity before the writing phase begins.

P2
10
min
Individual Commitment Writing
Silent, individual

Each person writes one commitment. Offer the sentence starter "I commit to…" as an anchor. The writing is silent. No discussion until all commitments have been written and the group is ready to share.

  • Optional: ask each person to also write a brief note on why this particular behavior — why they chose this one, what it costs them, or what they have seen happen when it is absent.
  • Use the prompt starters in the digital tool if the group needs a starting point. They are sentence fragments — each person finishes the thought in their own voice.
  • Allow 8–10 full minutes. Rushed commitments are generic ones.
Facilitator Note

Walk the room quietly during the writing phase. If someone is stuck, offer this: "Think about the last time this team fell short of its best. What one thing, if someone had done it differently, would have changed the outcome?" That is often where the most honest commitment lives.

P3
20
min
Share & Find Themes
Group activity

Each person reads their commitment aloud. After all commitments are read, the facilitator leads a brief clustering exercise: which commitments are pointing at the same underlying behavior? Group them on the theme board, in real time, as patterns emerge.

  • Do not interrupt or comment while someone reads their commitment. Listen first. Thank them. Move to the next person. Save all discussion for the theming phase.
  • In the theming phase, look for natural clusters: commitments about information sharing, commitments about accountability ownership, commitments about customer-first orientation, commitments about follow-through, commitments about raising uncomfortable truths.
  • A theme with four or more commitments pointing at it is almost certainly one of the team's most important — and most challenging — cultural pressure points. Name it explicitly.
Facilitator Note

The themes that cluster around negative space — around what the team is not doing — are more important than the ones that cluster around aspiration. If eight people independently commit to "speaking up when something is going wrong," the team is telling you something about what hasn't been safe to do. Sit with that for a moment before moving on.

P4
15
min
Draft the Shared Commitments
Collaborative writing

From the themes identified, the group drafts 3–5 shared commitments for the Culture Contract. Each shared commitment synthesizes a theme into a single, specific, first-person-plural behavioral statement that everyone can recognize in their own individual commitment.

  • Shared commitments begin with "We commit to…" and describe an observable team behavior, not an aspiration. "We commit to transparency" is aspirational. "We commit to sharing information that changes another function's risk picture within 24 hours of learning it" is behavioral.
  • Every person in the room should be able to look at each shared commitment and see their own individual commitment reflected in it. If someone can't, the synthesis is incomplete.
  • Draft in the session. Do not assign to a subgroup to finish later — the language that comes from the room in the moment carries a different weight than language polished offline.
P5
10
min
Sign the Contract
Commitment close

The facilitator reads the full contract aloud — preamble, shared commitments, and the accountability clause. Each person signs. The signing is not ceremonial. It is the moment the commitments become mutual obligations rather than good intentions.

  • Before signing, ask: "Is there anything in this contract you cannot honestly commit to?" Give genuine space for this question. A contract signed under social pressure is not a contract — it is compliance theater.
  • Agree on how the contract will be revisited. A 90-day check-in is standard: have we kept these commitments? Where have we fallen short? What needs to change?
  • Distribute the signed contract to every member of the team within 24 hours. It should live somewhere visible — not in a shared drive folder no one opens.
Facilitator Note

The most important thing you can do after the session is reference the contract. The first time a leader says, in a team meeting, "I want to check in on our culture contract commitment about X — how have we been doing?" the contract becomes real. Without that reference, it becomes a document.

CXKonnect Culture Contract
Our Commitments as One Team
Drafted and signed in session — reviewed every 90 days

We are a CX organization. Our measure of success is not the work we deliver — it is the outcome our customers achieve because of the work we deliver. This contract reflects the specific behaviors we commit to, in public, to each other, so that our team functions as one unit in service of that measure. We hold each other to these commitments with curiosity, not judgment — and we return to them when we fall short.

5
Discussion Prompts
During share-out
Q
Is the behavior you just committed to something you already do — or something you know you need to do more consistently? Distinguishes between aspirational commitments and honest ones. The most valuable commitments come from the second answer.
Q
If every person on this team kept this commitment for 90 days, what would a customer notice — specifically? Connects behavioral commitments to customer outcomes. If the answer is "nothing," the commitment may be internally focused rather than outcome-oriented.
Q
When is the moment in a typical week when this commitment would be hardest to keep? What makes it hard? Grounds the commitment in reality. Commitments that seem easy in the room are often the ones that quietly disappear under pressure.
Q
What would you need from this team in order to keep your commitment — what would make it easier or harder? Opens the reciprocal dimension. Culture contracts work when commitments are mutual — when keeping yours depends in part on others keeping theirs.
Q
How will we call each other back to this contract without it feeling like blame — what language or ritual will we use? The most practical closing question. Agreements without accountability mechanisms are wishes. This question designs the mechanism.
6
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • The final signed Culture Contract with every person's name
  • Every individual commitment card — these are the raw material that produced the shared commitments
  • The theme clustering — which themes clustered most densely, and what that tells you about the team's cultural pressure points
  • The agreed accountability ritual — how the team will reference the contract going forward
  • The next review date, confirmed and calendared before anyone leaves the room
After the session
What happens next
  • Distribute the signed contract to all participants within 24 hours
  • Post it somewhere the team sees regularly — a Slack channel pinned message, a recurring meeting document header, a physical printout in a shared space
  • At the first team meeting after the session, reference one commitment explicitly — create the precedent that this document is alive, not archived
  • At the 90-day review: read each commitment aloud, discuss honestly where it was kept and where it wasn't, and decide whether to renew, revise, or retire each one
  • When onboarding a new team member, share the contract on their first week — it is the most efficient introduction to how this team operates at its best
CXKonnect Principle

The Culture Contract is not a values exercise. It is a behavioral agreement. The gap between a CX organization that functions as one team and one that functions as adjacent teams is almost always a gap in explicit behavioral commitments — in what people have publicly agreed to do with and for each other, and in whether those agreements have been honored in practice. This exercise closes that gap — one commitment at a time, one team at a time, one honest signature at a time.
