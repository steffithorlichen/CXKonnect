Leadership Exercise

The Outcome Audit

Each leader articulates what outcome their two most important customers are hired to achieve — then compares across the team.

45 min
Leadership team
4–8 people
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🔍
Purpose
Expose Outcome Drift
Reveal whether your leadership team shares a common understanding of what outcomes customers are actually trying to achieve — or whether each function has quietly drifted toward its own definition.
👥
Who Should Attend
CX Leadership Team
All CX function leaders: CS, PS, Support, Education, Consulting, Managed Services. Every person who shapes how their team defines customer success.
📤
Output
Shared Outcome Language
A set of agreed-upon customer outcome statements that every CX function uses as the north star for how it defines and measures its own success.
🎯

Core Objective

The Outcome Audit starts from a deceptively simple premise: every leader on your team believes they know what their customers are trying to achieve. This exercise tests that belief by making it visible — and comparing it. The gaps between what different functions believe customers are hired to do are the gaps that produce misaligned priorities, misaligned metrics, and customers who feel like each team they talk to works for a different company.

1
Facilitation Guide
45 min total
P1
5
min
Set the Frame
Facilitator-led

Open by distinguishing between what customers use and what they are hired to achieve. A hospital network uses your platform — but they are hired to reduce patient readmission rates. A retailer uses your analytics tool — but they are hired to protect margin during promotional seasons. The product is the vehicle. The outcome is the destination.

  • Ask the group: "When your two most strategically important customers renew, what do they tell their own leadership team they got from us?" That answer is the outcome. It is almost never the feature list.
  • Clarify the format: each leader writes two customer outcome statements — one per customer — before any discussion. Statements must be in the customer's language, not the vendor's.
  • Distribute the individual worksheets (or open the digital version below). No discussion until all leaders have written their statements.
Facilitator Note

"Hired to achieve" is not the same as "happy with." A customer can be happy with your service and still not be achieving the outcome that justifies the cost of the contract. The audit asks about achievement, not satisfaction.

P2
10
min
Individual Outcome Writing
Silent, individual

Each leader completes their worksheet independently. For each of their two nominated customers, they write: the customer name or segment, the business outcome they believe that customer is hired to achieve, and the single most important evidence that would tell them the customer had achieved it.

  • Outcome statements must be specific. "Improve efficiency" is not an outcome. "Reduce the average time clinical staff spend on documentation by 30% without increasing error rates" is an outcome.
  • Evidence statements must be observable. "Customer feels good" is not evidence. "Customer's executive sponsor presents a 12-month ROI case to their board" is evidence.
  • If a leader cannot write a specific outcome statement for their most important customer, that itself is a finding worth naming in the debrief.
Facilitator Note

Silence is essential. If leaders discuss before writing, they will anchor to each other's framing. The value of the exercise lies in the uninfluenced first answer — what each function leader genuinely believes, based on their function's vantage point, before social pressure toward consensus has a chance to operate.

P3
15
min
Read Aloud & Map Alignment
Group activity

Each leader reads their outcome statements aloud for each customer. The facilitator plots alignment on the shared matrix as statements are read — marking Aligned (same or substantially similar), Partial (overlapping but emphasizing different dimensions), or Drifted (substantively different in ways that would produce different team priorities).

  • Do not interrupt or debate during the reading phase. Each leader reads their full statements before the group responds. Record everything before discussing anything.
  • Pay particular attention to the evidence statements. Leaders who name the same customer outcome but different evidence are often running different success models without realizing it.
  • Note any customer for whom a majority of leaders could not produce a specific outcome statement. This is the highest-risk account in the portfolio — serving it well is structurally impossible if no one knows what "well" means.
Facilitator Note

The most revealing moment in this exercise is often not the disagreements — it is the silences. When a CS leader reads a customer outcome statement and three other function leaders have no idea that customer had that goal, the room understands the problem immediately.

P4
10
min
Diagnose the Drifts
Group discussion

Focus exclusively on the Drifted and Partial cells. For each one, ask two questions: where did the different outcome definition come from, and what would each function have done differently for this customer if they had been working from the other function's definition?

  • Drift is almost never the result of indifference. It is usually the result of each function optimizing for the aspect of the customer relationship it is closest to — delivery quality, support satisfaction, training completion — without a shared north star that connects all of those to the outcome that actually matters to the customer.
  • Identify whether the drift is a knowledge gap (one function simply did not know what the customer needed) or a definition gap (functions have been using different vocabulary for success). Both are fixable, but differently.
  • Do not spend time assigning blame. The question is not who caused the drift. The question is what shared definition would have prevented it.
P5
5
min
Draft Shared Outcome Statements
Synthesis close

For every customer reviewed, the group agrees on a single shared outcome statement in the customer's language. This becomes the official outcome definition that every function leader takes back to their team. It does not have to be perfect — it has to be shared.

  • The statement must be specific enough that every function can derive a different but connected success metric from it. Generic outcomes produce generic metrics.
  • Assign one leader to document and distribute the agreed statements within 48 hours.
  • Set a 90-day check-in to ask: have any function-level priorities shifted as a result of working from the shared outcome definition?
Facilitator Note

Close by asking each leader to name one thing their function will do differently in the next 30 days as a result of what they heard in the session. Public commitment in the room is part of the change mechanism.

4
Insight Questions
During & after share-out
Specificity Check
Could a CSM use your outcome statement to decide what to prepare for next week's QBR?
If no — the statement is too generic to anchor team behavior. Rewrite until the answer is yes.
Customer Voice Check
Would the customer recognize this outcome statement as their own — in their own language?
If the statement sounds like your product's value proposition rather than the customer's business goal, it is vendor language, not outcome language.
Drift Source
For each drifted pair — when did the two functions last have a direct conversation with this customer about what success looks like?
Drift is rarely ideological. It is usually a recency problem. The function closest to the customer most recently shapes the outcome definition by default.
Metric Consequence
If functions are working from different outcome definitions, what metrics has each function been tracking — and what has each been ignoring?
The downstream consequence of outcome drift is metric misalignment. This question makes that concrete.
5
Discussion Prompts
Facilitation
Q
For the customers where we drifted — if we had been working from a shared outcome definition for the past six months, what would we have done differently? Makes the cost of drift concrete without assigning blame. Forces the group to think forward from what they just learned.
Q
Which function in this room is closest to the customer's actual outcome — and does that function have a mechanism to share what it knows with every other function? Surfaces the knowledge concentration problem. The function with the best customer insight often has no structural way to distribute it.
Q
Is the customer's outcome we just agreed on actually achievable with the way we currently operate — or does it require something we don't yet have in place? The most important honesty question. Shared outcome language only creates value if the organization can actually deliver against it.
Q
How does each function's current performance review measure its contribution to the shared customer outcome we just defined? Connects the outcome statement to incentive structures. If no function's performance review references the shared outcome, it will not survive the quarter.
Q
If we ran this exercise with the customer's own leadership team in the room — would their outcome statement match ours? The ultimate alignment question. Closes the loop between internal consensus and external reality.
6
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • Agreed shared outcome statement for every customer reviewed
  • Every drifted cell with a named root cause (knowledge gap or definition gap)
  • One behavior change each leader commits to in the next 30 days
  • Owner and deadline for distributing the agreed outcome statements
  • Date of 90-day check-in to review whether priorities have shifted
After the session
What to do with it
  • Distribute agreed outcome statements to every function within 48 hours
  • Review each function's current performance metrics against the shared outcome definition — identify what to add, change, or retire
  • Bring the shared outcome statements to the next QBR with each reviewed customer and ask: "Does this reflect what you're trying to achieve?"
  • Run the Outcome Audit quarterly for key accounts — customer outcomes evolve
  • Use drifted pairs as an input to the Gap Map Exercise — outcome drift and handoff gaps are almost always co-located
CXKonnect Principle

A CX organization that cannot state, in the customer's language, what its most important customers are hired to achieve is not a customer-centered organization — it is a service-delivery organization. The Outcome Audit is the first step in the transition between the two. Run it until the answer is never in doubt.
