The Gap Map

Teams map every customer handoff point and identify where context is lost between functions. Surfaces invisible cross-functional gaps.

60 min
6–10 people
Cross-functional
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🎯
Purpose
Surface Hidden Gaps
Make invisible cross-functional failures visible, nameable, and ownable before the next customer falls through them.
👥
Who Should Attend
All CX Function Leads
CS, PS, Support, Education, Consulting, Managed Services, Field Support, Partner Mgmt. No delegates — principals only.
📤
Output
Scored Gap Map
A prioritized list of critical handoff gaps, each with a named owner and a 30-day fix deadline.
💡

Core Objective

The most costly CX failures don't happen inside departments — they happen between them. This exercise gives your leadership team a shared, honest picture of where customer context is being lost at every functional handoff, and produces the structural accountability to fix it.

Facilitation Guide
60 min total
P1
10
min
Set the Frame
Facilitator-led

Open with the core premise: this exercise is not an evaluation of any function's internal performance. It is an evaluation of the connections between functions — specifically, whether each function reliably equips the next function to succeed at the point of handoff.

  • Explain the three scores: Red (critical gap — customer is falling through), Amber (weak — functional but inconsistent), Green (solid — documented, monitored, reliable).
  • Clarify that each cell is directional: the row function is the sender, the column function is the receiver. A red cell means the sender is not reliably setting up the receiver for success.
  • Emphasize that discrepancies between sender and receiver scores are the most valuable data — not a problem, but a finding.
  • Distribute individual scoring sheets. No discussion yet.
Facilitator Note

Name the resistance before it surfaces: "You may feel tempted to score your outgoing handoffs generously and incoming ones critically. Do the opposite — score from the receiving function's experience, not your own confidence in what you sent."

P2
15
min
Independent Scoring
Silent, individual

Each participant scores every handoff cell they are involved in — both as sender and receiver — without discussion. This silent period is essential. Uninfluenced first impressions are the most accurate signal the exercise produces.

  • Score only the cells where your function is the sender or the receiver. Leave cells you have no direct knowledge of blank.
  • For each cell, ask: "If I were the customer crossing this handoff today, what would I experience?" — not "how hard did we try?"
  • Mark N/A for functional pairs with no meaningful handoff in your organization. Blank ≠ N/A — blank means "I don't have enough information to score this."
Facilitator Note

Enforce the silence. If people start talking, redirect: "Hold your observations — we'll discuss everything in the next phase." The contrast between what people assume others will say and what they actually say is part of the exercise's value.

P3
20
min
Reveal & Discuss Discrepancies
Group discussion

Compile scores onto the shared master matrix and display the composite. Focus exclusively on discrepancies — cells where sender and receiver scored differently by more than one level. These are the most important conversations in the exercise.

  • For each discrepant cell, ask the sender: "What are you confident you are delivering at this handoff?" Then ask the receiver: "What are you actually receiving?" Listen for the gap between those two answers.
  • Do not adjudicate or assign blame. Capture the discrepancy as a finding and move on. The goal of this phase is discovery, not resolution.
  • Note any cells that every participant scored consistently red — these are structural failures, not perception gaps, and they likely represent the highest-priority fixes.
Facilitator Note

Time-box each cell discussion to 2 minutes. The most important cells will surface naturally — don't let a single contentious handoff consume the session. Park detailed debates on a "parking lot" sticky note and return to them in the action phase.

P4
10
min
Prioritize by Customer Impact
Group decision

Not all gaps require the same urgency. Rank the red cells by two criteria: the number of customers affected by this handoff and the severity of the downstream outcome impact when it fails.

  • Ask for each red cell: "How many customers cross this handoff per quarter?" and "When this handoff fails, what is the worst realistic customer outcome?"
  • Rank the red cells: Priority 1 (fix in 30 days), Priority 2 (fix in 60 days), Priority 3 (monitor and fix when resources allow).
  • Amber cells with large customer volume should be considered Priority 2 even if they are not critical — degraded handoffs compound over time.
P5
5
min
Assign Gap Owners
Accountability close

Every Priority 1 and Priority 2 gap must leave the room with a single named owner and a specific deliverable. A joint working group is not an owner. The owner is the person who will be accountable in the next session for whether the gap has closed.

  • For each Priority 1 cell: name the owner, define the deliverable (shared handoff standard, joint review cadence, escalation path documentation), set the deadline (30 days).
  • The owner should be the function leader with the most direct control over the root cause — usually the sender, unless the gap is caused by the receiver's intake process.
  • Schedule the follow-up session before leaving the room. The Gap Map has no value without the accountability check-in.
Facilitator Note

Close by re-reading the red cell list and its owners aloud. The public commitment in the room is part of the accountability mechanism. Do not skip it.

4
Discussion Prompts
Facilitation
Q
Where in this map do we have the clearest evidence that a customer has recently experienced the gap we just scored? Grounds the exercise in real customer impact. Prevents abstract scoring from disconnecting from lived experience.
Q
For the cells where the sender scored green and the receiver scored red — what does the sender believe they are delivering that the receiver is not receiving? The most productive discrepancy question. Usually reveals a missing shared definition of "complete handoff."
Q
Which gap, if closed in the next 30 days, would have the largest positive impact on the customer's experience — not our team's experience? Prevents the group from prioritizing gaps that are organizationally convenient to fix rather than most impactful to customers.
Q
What information does the receiving function need at handoff that it is not currently getting — and why has no one asked for it explicitly until now? Surfaces the difference between missing shared data and missing shared accountability for sharing it.
Q
If a new customer were joining us today and crossing every handoff on this map in their first 90 days, what would their experience be? Describe it honestly. Forces the group to read the map as a customer journey, not a functional operations review.
Q
What would a green score in our two highest-priority red cells actually look like in practice — what would the sender be doing differently, and what would the receiver be experiencing differently? Moves the group from problem identification to solution design without leaving the session.
5
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
What to capture
Before leaving the room
  • A photograph or export of the completed Gap Map
  • Every red cell with a named owner and specific deliverable
  • The three most significant discrepancies between sender and receiver scores
  • Any parking lot items that need a separate working session
  • The date, time, and attendees for the 30-day follow-up session
What happens next
30-day follow-up
  • Each gap owner presents their deliverable — the documented handoff standard, shared signal, or joint review cadence
  • Re-score the cells that were addressed — did the fix produce a genuine improvement?
  • Escalate any unresolved Priority 1 gaps to CX leadership as a structural design issue
  • Set the date for the next full Gap Map exercise (recommended: annually)
  • Distribute the updated map to every CX function as a shared organizational record
CXKonnect Principle

Run the Gap Map Exercise once per year at minimum. Cross-functional gaps are not static — they shift as teams grow, change leadership, and adopt new tools. A handoff that was solid last year may have degraded silently. The map is not a permanent document. It is an annual reckoning with the invisible seams in your organization — and the customers who fall through them.
