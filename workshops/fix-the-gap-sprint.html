Fix the Gap Sprint

Cross-functional pairs map one transition in the customer journey and design a minimum viable handoff protocol together.

Cross-functional

Activity Overview
🧩
Purpose
Build the Missing Protocol
Pairs of function leads don't just identify what's broken at their shared handoff — they spend the bulk of the session designing the minimum viable protocol to fix it. Output is a draft, not a discussion.
👥
Who Should Attend
Cross-Functional Pairs
3–6 pairs of function leads, each owning a different customer journey transition. Both sides of every handoff must be in the pair. No observers — everyone produces a protocol.
📤
Output
Draft MVH Protocols
A minimum viable handoff protocol per pair — one transition, one agreed customer outcome, one checklist, one verification signal, and one named owner per side. Ready to pilot in 30 days.
⚙️

Core Objective

Most cross-functional workshops produce agreements about what needs to happen. The Fix the Gap Sprint produces the thing itself. Each pair of function leads is given 60 minutes to draft a minimum viable handoff protocol for their specific transition — the smallest, most implementable set of standards that would meaningfully improve the customer's experience at that handoff point. Minimum viable means it can be piloted in 30 days, not planned for a quarter.

1
Facilitation Guide
90 min total
P1
10
min
Frame & Pair Assignment
Full group

Open with the core premise: each pair owns one customer journey transition — the moment when the customer moves from one function's care to another's. The pair's job is not to discuss the gap. It is to design the protocol that closes it.

  • Introduce the concept of a Minimum Viable Handoff (MVH): the smallest set of standards that, if consistently applied, would meaningfully improve the customer's experience at this specific transition. Not the ideal handoff. The minimum viable one — implementable in 30 days, pilotable with real accounts.
  • Assign pairs from the suggested list or build custom pairs from the customer journey transitions most in need of a fix. Each pair should represent two functions with a known or suspected handoff gap.
  • Confirm that both sides of every pair are present — the person who currently sends the customer away and the person who currently receives them. A pair with only one function is a monologue, not a design session.
Facilitator Note

Pairs should be co-located (same physical or breakout space) for the working session. Pairs that work across a shared screen produce better protocols than pairs that email each other. If remote, assign one dedicated video breakout room per pair for the 60-minute working block.

P2
15
min
Map the Current Transition
Pairs — concurrent

Each pair maps their current transition honestly before designing anything new. This phase is deliberately short — 15 minutes — to prevent the mapping from consuming the design time. The point is to agree on the problem before solving it.

  • Each pair member answers three questions independently (2 min each), then compares: What does the customer experience at this transition today? What information is typically lost or incomplete at the point of handoff? What is the most common customer complaint or confusion in the 30 days after this transition?
  • After comparing, agree on a single problem statement: "At this transition, customers consistently experience _____ because _____." This problem statement anchors the protocol design.
  • If the pair disagrees on the problem statement, that disagreement is the most important finding of the session — name it explicitly before proceeding.
Facilitator Note

Check in on each pair at the 12-minute mark. If they are still mapping and haven't agreed on a problem statement, redirect: "Choose the problem you most agree on and start designing. You can name the open disagreement in your protocol as an assumption to test."

P3
40
min
Design the Minimum Viable Handoff Protocol
Pairs — concurrent

The core working block. Each pair uses the Protocol Canvas to design their MVH — the minimum viable set of standards that would meaningfully improve the customer's experience at this transition. The constraint is implementability: every element of the protocol must be pilotable in 30 days without new tools, significant budget, or organizational approval beyond the two people in the room.

  • The protocol has three components: what the sending function commits to delivering before the handoff (the pre-conditions), what the handoff itself looks like in practice (the trigger, the channel, the content), and what the receiving function commits to confirming within 48 hours of handoff (the verification).
  • The MVH checklist captures the minimum items the sending function must have completed — and the receiving function must have confirmed — for the handoff to be considered complete by both sides.
  • The "minimum viable" test: if you added one more item to this checklist, would you still be able to pilot it with real accounts in 30 days? If no, remove something. Keep removing until the answer is yes.
Facilitator Note

The most common failure mode in this phase is scope inflation — pairs designing the ideal handoff rather than the minimum viable one. If a pair has more than 7 items on their checklist at the 25-minute mark, intervene: "Which three items on this list, if only these three were in place, would most meaningfully improve the customer's experience? Start there. Everything else is Version 2."

P4
20
min
Gallery Share — Protocols on the Wall
Full group

Each pair presents their MVH protocol to the full group in 3 minutes: one slide or canvas summary — the transition they mapped, the problem statement they agreed on, the three core protocol elements, and the 30-day pilot commitment. The group asks one clarifying question per presentation, then moves on.

  • The gallery format creates peer accountability — each pair's protocol is visible and comparable to every other pair's. Pairs that produced aspirational rather than minimum viable protocols will recognize this when they hear what other pairs designed in the same time.
  • After all pairs present, the full group identifies the one protocol that, if implemented first, would most improve the customer's experience across the whole journey. This is the cohort's top priority.
  • Note any protocol elements that could apply across multiple handoffs — these are candidates for an organization-wide handoff standard, not just a single-pair protocol.
Facilitator Note

Time-box presentations hard. 3 minutes per pair, 1 minute for one question. With 4 pairs this is 16 minutes — leaving 4 minutes for the group priority vote. A presentation that runs long is a signal that the protocol wasn't distilled enough. Name it generously: "Let's capture the rest in the canvas — the constraint is the 3 minutes, not the quality of the work."

P5
5
min
Commit to the 30-Day Pilot
Accountability close

Close with each pair naming their 30-day pilot commitment: which accounts will they apply the protocol to, who is responsible for the sending side and who for the receiving side, and what observable customer signal will tell them whether the protocol is working?

  • A pilot with a named customer set and a defined observable signal is a real pilot. A pilot described as "we'll start using this" is an intention. Name the difference explicitly, and push every pair toward the former before the session closes.
  • Schedule the 30-day retrospective — when all pairs reconvene, present what they observed in the pilot, and either ratify the protocol for full deployment or revise based on what they learned.
  • Close by reading each pair's protocol commitment aloud — pair name, transition, three core protocol elements, pilot accounts, and observable signal. The public commitment is part of the accountability mechanism.

5
Discussion Prompts
For pairs & full group
Q
If you applied this protocol to your last five handoffs exactly as written — would all five customers have had a better experience? Which one would still have fallen through, and why? Tests whether the protocol addresses the actual failure pattern or just the most recent visible problem. A protocol that doesn't hold against past failures needs revision.
Q
What does the customer experience in the first 48 hours after this handoff — specifically, what question do they most commonly have that neither function is currently answering proactively? Identifies the most common post-handoff customer confusion and ensures the protocol addresses it explicitly rather than assuming it will resolve itself.
Q
Which item on your protocol checklist is the one both sides would most want to skip when under pressure? That item needs either a stronger accountability mechanism or a simpler design. Protocol items that disappear under pressure are the ones with inadequate accountability or excessive friction. The most important items need the simplest execution paths.
Q
After the gallery share: which element from another pair's protocol could be adopted by every function pair in this room — regardless of the specific transition it was designed for? Surfaces universally applicable protocol components that could become an organization-wide handoff standard, not just a single-pair solution.
Q
When you pilot this protocol in the next 30 days — what customer signal will tell you unambiguously whether it is working? Not a process signal. A customer experience signal. Forces the observable customer signal to be named before the pilot begins. Protocols without a customer-facing success signal produce activity measurement, not outcome measurement.
6
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • Every pair's completed Protocol Canvas — transition, problem statement, three protocol elements, MVH checklist
  • Each pair's 30-day pilot commitment: named accounts, owner per side, observable customer signal
  • The group's top-priority protocol — the one that, if implemented first, most improves the customer journey
  • Any protocol elements identified as candidates for an organization-wide standard
  • The date, attendees, and format for the 30-day retrospective
After the session
What happens next
  • Each pair distributes their protocol to both function teams within 24 hours — it should exist as a shared document, not only in a session recording
  • Each pair applies the protocol to their named pilot accounts for 30 days — documenting exceptions, friction points, and customer observations
  • At the 30-day retrospective: each pair presents what they observed, what they changed, and whether the observable customer signal moved
  • Protocols that passed the 30-day pilot are ratified as the standing handoff standard for that transition and documented in the organization's process library
  • Protocols that failed or needed significant revision are redesigned using what the pilot taught — and re-piloted for another 30 days before ratification
CXKonnect Principle

The handoff is the organization made visible. Every gap between CX functions is eventually experienced by a customer — as confusion, as friction, as the feeling of being passed between strangers. The Fix the Gap Sprint doesn't produce a plan to fix that feeling someday. It produces a protocol to fix it in 30 days, in the specific transition where it is most acute, with the specific people who own it. That is the unit of organizational change — not the strategy, not the framework, not the aspiration. One handoff. Two functions. A draft protocol. A 30-day pilot. Repeat until the customer journey is seamless.
