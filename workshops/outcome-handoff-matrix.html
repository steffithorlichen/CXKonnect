The Outcome Handoff Matrix

Each function lead writes what the customer should experience after their work is done. Mismatches reveal where standards need alignment.

60 min
Cross-functional
All functions
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🤝
Purpose
Align Handoff Standards
Expose the gap between what each function believes it is delivering at handoff and what the next function — and more critically, the customer — is actually experiencing.
👥
Who Should Attend
All CX Function Leads
Every function that touches the customer in your organization — CS, PS, Support, Education, Consulting, Managed Services, Field Support, Partner Mgmt. No delegates.
📤
Output
Shared Handoff Standards
A set of agreed-upon customer experience statements at every function handoff point, with mismatches named, owned, and assigned a resolution path.
🔗

Core Objective

Every function in your CX organization has an implicit standard for what "done" looks like — and those standards almost never align across the handoff point. A PS team that considers go-live complete when the system is technically live, and a CS team that considers handoff complete when the customer's champion is ready to own adoption, are working to different definitions of the same moment. This exercise makes those implicit standards explicit — and forces the group to agree on a shared one.

1
Facilitation Guide
60 min total
P1
5
min
Set the Frame — The Customer at the Handoff
Facilitator-led

Open with the central question: when your function's work is complete and the customer transitions to the next function — what should the customer be experiencing at that exact moment? Not what you have delivered. Not what the next function should now do. What the customer's actual experience should be.

  • Distinguish between output ("we completed the implementation") and outcome experience ("the customer's team is confident, their primary workflow is running, and they have a named contact for the first 30 days"). The exercise asks only for outcome experience statements.
  • Clarify the writing format: each function lead writes one statement per handoff they own. The statement describes the customer's experience in first-person-customer language — what the customer would say if asked "how do you feel about where you are right now?"
  • Emphasize that mismatches are the goal, not a failure. Finding where two functions have different standards for the same handoff is precisely the value this exercise creates.
Facilitator Note

"What should the customer experience" is a different question than "what should the customer have received." Deliverables are outputs. Experiences are outcomes. If a function lead writes "the customer should have received a go-live checklist," redirect: "What should the customer feel and be able to do because of that checklist?"

P2
15
min
Independent Writing — Outcome Experience Statements
Silent, individual

Each function lead writes their outcome experience statements independently and silently — one statement per handoff they are involved in, from both the sending and receiving perspective. The silence is essential. Cross-contamination before writing produces socially acceptable statements, not honest ones.

  • For each handoff: write what the customer should be experiencing immediately after your function's work ends. Use the customer's perspective — "The customer should feel / know / be able to…"
  • Also write what you currently observe customers experiencing at that handoff — the gap between these two statements is the starting point for the discussion.
  • If you are unsure what the customer experiences at a handoff you own, name that uncertainty. It is a finding, not an excuse.
Facilitator Note

Walk the room quietly. If someone finishes early and looks disengaged, ask them: "What about the handoff that comes before yours? What does the customer experience when they arrive at your function — and does that match what the sending function intends?" This seeds the next phase.

P3
20
min
Read, Compare & Map Mismatches
Group activity

Each function lead reads their outcome experience statements aloud for every handoff they own. The facilitator plots the alignment in real time on the shared matrix — Match (both functions describe the same customer experience), Near (similar but meaningfully different in emphasis or timing), or Gap (the sending function's success state is the receiving function's problem state).

  • The most revealing moment is the Gap cell: when the function that sends a customer away considers the handoff successful at the exact moment the receiving function considers it a problem. This is not a miscommunication. It is a missing shared standard.
  • Pay close attention to timing mismatches — two functions with the same outcome standard but different assumptions about when it should be achieved are in a Near cell, not a Match. Timing is part of the standard.
  • Record every Gap and Near in the Mismatch Log as it is identified. Do not rely on memory — the specifics of each mismatch are the raw material for the fix.
Facilitator Note

When a Gap appears, pause before moving on. Ask both functions: "What is the customer experiencing in the space between your 'done' and your 'ready'?" That space — the gap between one function's completion and the next function's readiness — is where customers live. Making it visible is the most important thing this exercise does.

P4
15
min
Draft Shared Outcome Standards
Collaborative writing

For each Gap and Near cell identified, the two involved functions draft a shared outcome experience standard — a single agreed statement of what the customer should be experiencing at that handoff point, that both the sending and receiving function can hold themselves accountable to.

  • The shared standard must pass the "Day 1 test": could a new employee joining either function use this statement to understand what a successful handoff looks like, before any training? If not, it is still too abstract.
  • The standard must specify timing: "immediately after go-live" vs. "within 30 days of go-live" are not the same standard, even if the outcome language is identical.
  • Each shared standard needs a verification question — a single question you could ask the customer to confirm the standard was met. "On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you in your ability to operate the system independently?" is a verification question. "Are you happy?" is not.
P5
5
min
Assign Ownership & Close
Accountability close

Every Gap cell in the matrix must leave the room with a named owner — the person responsible for translating the shared outcome standard into an operational change in their function's process or playbook within 30 days.

  • The owner is typically the sending function's lead — they are the one who needs to change what "done" means before the handoff occurs.
  • Close by reading every shared outcome standard aloud — all of them, in sequence — as a single continuous description of what the customer should experience as they move through your organization. The full reading makes the collective standard vivid in a way that individual cells do not.
  • Schedule the 30-day check-in before leaving the room. The matrix is not a document — it is an accountability tool. Without a check-in, it becomes a record of a conversation.
Facilitator Note

The full sequential reading at the close is the most powerful moment in the exercise. When the group hears what the customer's complete journey should sound like — and compares it mentally to what it currently sounds like — the case for the work ahead becomes self-evident.

5
Discussion Prompts
During share-out
Q
For every Gap cell — what is the customer doing, feeling, or asking in the space between the sender's "done" and the receiver's "ready"? Makes the cost of the gap concrete and human. Replaces abstract standards language with a real customer moment.
Q
When did we last ask a customer directly what they experienced at this handoff — and would their answer match either of the standards we just wrote? Introduces the customer's voice as the arbiter. Both function standards may be wrong — the customer's experience is the only ground truth.
Q
For each shared standard we just drafted — what specifically does the sending function need to do differently for the customer to arrive at the receiving function in that state? Translates the standard into operational change. Standards without behavioral specifics remain aspirational.
Q
Which Gap in this matrix, if closed in the next 30 days, would most directly improve the customer's experience of moving through our organization? Forces prioritization when the matrix reveals multiple gaps simultaneously. Not all gaps have equal customer impact.
Q
What would it mean for our performance metrics — by function — if we held each other accountable to these shared standards? What would each function have to stop counting as a success? The most challenging closing question. Shared handoff standards often require each function to retire metrics that reward individual completion at the expense of collective outcome.
6
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • The completed Handoff Matrix — photographed or exported
  • Every shared outcome standard drafted in the session — verbatim
  • Every Gap cell with a named owner and a 30-day implementation deadline
  • The verification question for each shared standard — how will we confirm the customer is experiencing it?
  • The date of the 30-day check-in, confirmed and calendared
After the session
What happens next
  • Distribute the full matrix and shared standards to all function leads within 24 hours
  • Each Gap owner translates the shared standard into a specific process or playbook change within their function — not a communication plan, an operational change
  • At the 30-day check-in: each owner presents the change they made and how they verified it with a customer
  • Re-run the Matrix exercise every 6 months — handoff standards degrade as teams grow and change leadership
  • Use the shared standards as onboarding material for new function leads: this is how we define "done" at every handoff in this organization
CXKonnect Principle

The customer does not experience your functions. They experience the handoffs between them. Every function in a CX organization can be performing to its own standard of excellence while the customer travels through a series of disconnected experiences that add up to something far less than excellent. The Outcome Handoff Matrix exists to close that gap — by replacing implicit, misaligned, individual standards with explicit, agreed, shared ones. One handoff at a time.
