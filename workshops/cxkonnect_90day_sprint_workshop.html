The 90-Day Transformation Sprint

Leadership team defines a CX Konnect pilot: one journey, one segment, one success metric. Assigns ownership and milestones.

90 min
Leadership
4–8 people
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🚀
Purpose
From Framework to Flight
Convert the CXKonnect framework from a leadership commitment into a live pilot with a specific scope, a single success metric, and named ownership at every milestone.
👥
Who Should Attend
CX Leadership Team
The leaders who will own the pilot — CS, PS, Support, or a cross-functional group — plus the sponsor who has the organizational authority to clear obstacles in the 90-day window.
📤
Output
A Signed Sprint Canvas
One pilot defined at full specificity: journey, segment, metric, three milestone sets, a named owner per milestone, and a readiness assessment for each phase.
⚡

Core Objective

The hardest part of any transformation is the moment between strategy and action — when the framework is understood but nothing has changed yet. This session closes that gap by forcing three decisions that most leadership teams avoid: scope (not everything, one thing), metric (not five things, one thing), and ownership (not the team, one person). A 90-day sprint with those three decisions made is a transformation. Without them, it is a plan.

Facilitation Guide
90 min total
P1
10

Set the Frame — One Pilot, Three Constraints
Facilitator-led

Open by naming the most common way transformation initiatives fail: they are defined at the level of the whole organization rather than at the level of one specific change. The 90-Day Sprint is deliberately constrained to one journey, one segment, and one metric — not because those are the only things that matter, but because focus is the only condition under which genuine change is observable and learnable.

  • One journey: a specific customer lifecycle moment where the CXKonnect framework would change how the organization operates. Not "the whole customer journey" — one handoff, one stage transition, one outcome gap.
  • One segment: the customer cohort this pilot will apply to. Not "all enterprise customers" — a specific tier, vertical, or account set where the results will be visible within 90 days.
  • One metric: the single number that will tell you, on Day 91, whether the pilot worked. Not a dashboard. One number that changes if the transformation is real.
Facilitator Note

Expect resistance to the constraints. "We can't do just one metric" or "one segment isn't representative" are how leadership teams delay commitment. Name this directly: "The constraint isn't permanent. It's how we prove the concept before we scale it. What one metric, if it moved, would make every person in this room believe the framework is working?"

P2
20
min
Define the Pilot Scope
Group decision

The group selects the journey, segment, and metric using the pilot definition tool. This is the most important decision in the session — every milestone that follows depends on the specificity of the scope. Spend time here. Vague scope produces vague milestones and invisible results.

  • For the journey: choose the moment in the customer lifecycle where your organization's performance is most inconsistent, or where a CXKonnect outcome stage transition is currently stalling most often.
  • For the segment: choose a cohort large enough to produce statistically meaningful results in 90 days, but small enough that the pilot team can maintain deep visibility into every account in scope.
  • For the metric: choose the leading indicator most directly connected to the journey you selected. If the journey is the PS-to-CS handoff, the metric is 90-day post-go-live health score. If the journey is adoption, the metric is feature adoption depth at 60 days.
Facilitator Note

The metric must be something you can measure today. If you don't currently track it, the first milestone in the sprint is building the measurement capability — not the transformation work itself. Name this if it surfaces: "If we can't measure it now, our Day 1 milestone is making it measurable. That's still a valid sprint."

P3
30
min
Build the Milestone Canvas
Structured planning

With scope defined, the group populates the Sprint Canvas with milestones for each 30-day phase. Each phase has a different character: Days 1–30 are about building the foundation (data, baseline, signal infrastructure), Days 31–60 are about running the pilot (first interventions, first results, first learning), Days 61–90 are about proving and scaling (documented outcomes, expansion decision).

  • Each milestone must have a named owner — one person, not a team. The owner is accountable for reporting progress at the weekly sprint check-in.
  • Milestones must be testable: a yes/no completion condition, not a percentage of effort. "Baseline health scores documented for all 40 pilot accounts" is testable. "Work on health scoring baseline" is not.
  • Aim for 3–4 milestones per phase. More than 4 indicates the scope is too broad for a single sprint — push back and narrow.
Facilitator Note

Watch for milestone inflation in Days 61–90. Groups often front-load ambition into the back third because it feels safe — it's the furthest away. Push back: "If Days 1–30 and 31–60 go exactly as planned, is this Day 90 milestone actually achievable? Or are we overbuilding the plan?"

P4
20
min
Readiness Check & Risk Surface
Honest assessment

Before finalizing the canvas, run a readiness check for each phase. The question is not "will we succeed?" It is "what must be true by Day 1 of each phase for its milestones to be achievable, and is that thing currently in place?" Every condition that is not currently in place is a pre-sprint risk that needs an owner and a resolution path before the sprint launches.

  • Common Day 1–30 pre-conditions: data availability (can we baseline the metric?), team capacity (do owners have bandwidth?), executive air cover (will the sponsor protect the pilot from competing priorities?).
  • Common Day 31–60 pre-conditions: playbook exists (do we know what intervention we are running?), cross-functional alignment (do all functions in scope agree on their role in the pilot?), feedback loop (how will we know if something isn't working before Day 60?).
  • If more than three pre-conditions are unmet, the sprint launch date should be pushed back until they are resolved. A sprint that launches without its foundations is a performance review, not a transformation.
P5
10
min
Name the Sprint & Launch Date
Commitment close

Close the session by naming the sprint — a short, specific title that captures what the pilot is doing — and confirming the launch date. These two acts convert the canvas from a planning document into an organizational commitment. The sprint name should be sayable in a Slack message. The launch date should be within 30 days of today.

  • Name examples: "PS Handoff Sprint — Enterprise Segment Q2," "Adoption Acceleration — Healthcare Tier 1," "Proactive Engagement Pilot — At-Risk Accounts." Specific enough to describe the work. Short enough to become shorthand.
  • Confirm the weekly sprint check-in cadence before leaving the room — who attends, who facilitates, how progress is reported, and what decision authority the check-in carries if a milestone is at risk.
  • Name the Day 90 decision: if the pilot meets its success metric, what happens next? Expansion to a new segment? Full rollout? Naming the success scenario in advance is what converts a pilot into a strategic proof point rather than a one-off project.
Facilitator Note

Read the completed canvas aloud before closing — sprint name, scope, metric, all milestones with owners, and the Day 90 decision. The public recitation of the full plan in the room is part of the commitment mechanism. It makes the plan real in a way that a shared document alone does not.

4
Discussion Prompts
When the group stalls
Q
If we could only prove the CXKonnect framework works in one place in the next 90 days, where would we choose — and why would that proof matter most to this organization? Cuts through scope inflation. Forces the group to prioritize based on strategic leverage, not operational convenience.
Q
For each milestone we just assigned — is the owner actually available for this work in the next 30 days, or are we assigning it to someone who is already at capacity? The most important practical question. Sprint plans collapse on capacity, not strategy. Naming the capacity constraint before launch is the fix.
Q
What is the most likely reason this sprint stalls or fails — and what structural decision can we make in this room to prevent it? Pre-mortem framing. Identifying the most likely failure mode before launch is more valuable than any mitigation plan after the fact.
Q
If our Day 90 metric moves in the right direction, what decision will leadership make as a result — and do we have that commitment today, or are we building a proof point for a decision that hasn't been pre-authorized? Surfaces the political risk before it becomes a disappointment. Pilots without a pre-defined success pathway are energy expenditures, not transformation investments.
Q
What is the minimum version of this pilot that would still produce a meaningful proof point — and is that what we've designed, or have we over-engineered it? Identifies whether the sprint is optimally scoped or whether perfectionism has inflated it beyond what 90 days can deliver. Simpler is almost always more demonstrative.
5
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • The completed Sprint Canvas — scope, all milestones, all owners, readiness status
  • Every unmet pre-condition with an owner and a resolution date
  • The sprint launch date and weekly check-in cadence confirmed and calendared
  • The Day 90 decision — what happens if the metric moves, and who has authority to make that call
  • The sprint name — everyone in the room should be able to say it the same way
After the session
What happens next
  • Distribute the completed Sprint Canvas to all owners within 24 hours
  • Each owner confirms their milestone is correctly stated and their capacity is real — any correction surfaces before Day 1
  • At each weekly check-in: review milestone status (not progress — status: done, in progress, at risk), surface blockers, and make the minimum necessary decisions to unblock
  • At Day 30 and Day 60: run a 20-minute mini-retrospective — what is working, what is not, and what one thing will change in the next phase
  • At Day 90: present the sprint story — scope, metric movement, what changed, and the recommendation for what comes next — to the full CX leadership team and the executive sponsor
CXKonnect Principle

The 90-Day Sprint is not a test of whether the CXKonnect framework works. It is a proof of what your organization can do when it focuses. The framework provides the theory. The sprint provides the evidence. And the evidence — one specific number that moved in 90 days because your organization deliberately made it move — is what converts a leadership commitment into organizational belief. Start there. Start small. Start now.
