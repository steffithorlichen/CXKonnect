Stage Health Audit

Rate each of the five CX Konnect stages Red/Yellow/Green as a team. Identify root causes for every non-green stage.

60 min
Leadership team
4–8 people
In-person or remote
Activity Overview
🏥
Purpose
Honest Stage Diagnosis
Give the leadership team a shared, evidence-based picture of where your organization is performing at each CXKonnect outcome stage — and where the structural problems are hiding.
👥
Who Should Attend
CX Leadership Team
The leaders responsible for customer outcomes across CS, PS, Support, Education, and any other function that shapes a customer's ability to progress through the five stages.
📤
Output
Stage Ratings + Root Causes
A Red/Yellow/Green rating for each stage, a named root cause for every non-green rating, a prioritized action list, and a 30-day commitment per critical gap.
🔬

Core Objective

The five CXKonnect outcome stages describe a progression every customer should move through — from Activation to Advocacy. The Stage Health Audit asks a single uncomfortable question for each stage: are your customers actually progressing through this stage consistently, or is this stage where the journey quietly stalls? The answer, rated honestly and backed by evidence, tells you more about your CX organization's structural health than any dashboard.

1
Facilitation Guide
60 min total
P1
5
min
Set the Frame — Evidence, Not Opinion
Facilitator-led

Open by establishing the rating standard: a Green stage is not one where the team feels good about performance. It is one where the data confirms that customers are progressing through this stage consistently, at benchmark rates, without requiring exceptional effort from individual team members. Green is structural. Yellow is inconsistent. Red is broken.

  • Remind the group that ratings are about the population of customers — not the best examples or the worst. "We have some accounts in great shape at this stage" is not evidence of a green rating. It is evidence of inconsistency.
  • Clarify that the purpose is not to surface who is responsible for non-green stages. It is to identify the structural condition that makes consistent progression impossible — and agree on what needs to change.
  • Pre-warn: the most common conflict in this exercise is between the team's aspirational self-image and the data. When the data and the feeling disagree, the data wins.
Facilitator Note

Ask before you begin: "For each stage, what data do we currently have that tells us what percentage of customers are progressing through it consistently?" If the answer is "we don't have that data," that is a finding — and a yellow rating at minimum for that stage, regardless of how the team feels.

P2
5
min
Independent Rating — All Five Stages
Silent, individual

Each leader rates all five stages silently before any discussion. This silent first rating is the most important data the exercise produces — it captures what each leader actually believes before social pressure, seniority dynamics, or the desire to appear optimistic influences the result.

  • For each stage, rate Green, Yellow, or Red using the definitions provided. Do not average — choose the rating that most accurately describes the population of customers at this stage right now.
  • Also note, for each stage, the single piece of evidence that most influenced your rating. This prevents ratings without evidence and makes the debrief discussion concrete.
  • Allow 4–5 minutes. The ratings should be fast — if a leader is uncertain, that uncertainty itself is a finding.
P3
30
min
Stage-by-Stage Review & Root Cause
Structured group discussion

Work through all five stages in sequence. For each stage: reveal individual ratings, agree on a composite team rating, and — for every non-green stage — identify the root cause. Spend approximately 6 minutes per stage.

  • For Green stages: confirm the evidence. What specific data or observation backs the green rating? What would cause it to slip to yellow in the next 90 days if unattended? Spend 2 minutes maximum on green stages.
  • For Yellow stages: name the specific inconsistency. Is it a data gap (we don't know if customers are progressing)? A process gap (customers could progress but there's no consistent mechanism to help them)? A signal gap (we know something is wrong but nobody is acting on it)?
  • For Red stages: name the structural root cause. Not the symptom, not the story — the organizational design condition that makes consistent customer progression through this stage structurally unlikely.
Facilitator Note

If the group cannot agree on a rating, default to the worse rating. An organization where leaders disagree on whether a stage is Green or Yellow has a Yellow stage at best — the disagreement is itself evidence of inconsistency. The same logic applies to Yellow vs. Red: if anyone at the table has evidence for Red, the composite is Red until that evidence is addressed.

P4
15
min
Prioritize & Assign Actions
Group decision

With all five stages rated and root causes identified, the group prioritizes the non-green stages and assigns a named owner and a 30-day structural fix to each Red and Yellow rating.

  • Prioritize Red before Yellow. Within Reds, prioritize by customer impact — the stage that affects the most customers, or the stage where the root cause is most likely to produce churn in the next 90 days.
  • For each Red: assign a single owner with a specific structural change and a 30-day deadline. The fix is not a communication plan or a team conversation. It is a structural change — a new process, a new signal, a new shared standard, a new accountability mechanism.
  • For each Yellow: assign an owner to produce a measurement plan — how will we know with data whether this stage is improving or declining? Yellows that cannot be measured have no path to green.
P5
5
min
Read the Full Picture & Close
Synthesis close

Close by reading the complete stage health profile aloud — all five ratings, all root causes, all owners and deadlines — as a single continuous organizational diagnosis. Then ask the group: "If we came back in 90 days and every action was completed, what would this profile look like?" The answer is the target state.

  • Name the 90-day review date before leaving the room. The audit has no value without a follow-up that re-rates every non-green stage against the structural changes made.
  • Distribute the completed audit to every leader within 24 hours. It should be treated as a standing organizational document — not a one-off exercise output.
  • Commit to running this audit quarterly. Stage health shifts — green stages can degrade; red stages can improve. The audit is only valuable if it is repeated.
Facilitator Note

If the full reading reveals that three or more stages are Red or Yellow, resist the urge to expand the action list proportionally. Prioritize ruthlessly: two structural fixes made well in 90 days produce more organizational improvement than six fixes made partially. Depth beats breadth in transformation work.

5
Discussion Prompts
Stage-by-stage facilitation
Q
For each stage we just rated — what specific data do we have that tells us what percentage of our customers are progressing through it consistently? If we don't have that data, why not? The foundational question. Forces the group to distinguish between evidence-based ratings and feeling-based ones. No data = Yellow at minimum.
Q
For every Yellow stage — is the inconsistency random, or does it cluster by CSM, by segment, by function, or by time period? What does the pattern tell us about the root cause? Patterns in inconsistency reveal structural causes. Random inconsistency is a different problem from clustered inconsistency — and requires a different fix.
Q
For every Red stage — what would a customer experience if they could tell us exactly what is happening to them at this stage? What would they say is missing? Restores the customer to the center of the discussion. Root causes named in organizational language often become clearer when translated into customer experience language.
Q
Which stage, if improved from Red or Yellow to Green, would have the greatest downstream impact on the stages that follow it — and why? Prioritizes the fix sequence. Stage 1 problems compound through all subsequent stages. A Stage 3 problem may have limited downstream effect. Fixing in the right order multiplies impact.
Q
What would this audit look like if we ran it with our customers in the room — rating not their perception of our performance, but our actual structural ability to help them progress? Closes the loop between internal assessment and customer reality. Internal green ratings that customers would dispute are the most dangerous ones.
6
Debrief & Next Steps
Close-out
Before leaving the room
What to capture
  • The composite rating for all five stages — agreed, not averaged
  • The named root cause for every non-green stage
  • Every action in the priority log with a named owner and 30-day deadline
  • The 90-day target state — what this audit profile looks like if all actions are completed
  • The date of the quarterly re-audit, calendared before the room empties
After the session
What happens next
  • Distribute the completed audit to all leaders within 24 hours — stage ratings, root causes, owners, and deadlines
  • At each weekly leadership meeting: each owner reports on their action's status — not progress, status: on track / at risk / complete
  • At 30 days: review whether structural changes have been implemented. Ask: "Would the rating change if we ran the audit today?"
  • At 90 days: re-run the full audit. Compare the new ratings to this session's. The delta is the evidence of organizational improvement — or the evidence that the fixes didn't hold
  • Use the quarterly audit trend — four consecutive ratings per stage — as your primary indicator of CX organizational health for leadership reporting
CXKonnect Principle

The Stage Health Audit is not a performance review. It is an organizational design review. A red stage is not a failure of the people working at that stage — it is a failure of the structural conditions that determine whether customers can progress. The audit's job is to name those conditions clearly enough that someone in the room can own changing them. Run it quarterly. Be honest every time. The trend line is what matters.
