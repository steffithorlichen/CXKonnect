From Reaction to Connection: A Leader's Transition Guide

Your teams are not underperforming — they are unconnected. How to move your organization from reactive chaos to proactive clarity in 90 days.

Stefanie Thorlichen Dec 18, 2025 9 min read

There is a particular kind of exhaustion that settles into a CX organization that has been reactive for too long. It is not the exhaustion of people who are not working hard enough. It is the exhaustion of people who are working as hard as they know how — and still feel like they are losing. Fires get put out, and new ones start. Escalations get resolved, and different escalations take their place. The team is perpetually busy and perpetually behind, and no one can quite explain why, because on any given day the work looks reasonable.

The problem is not capacity. It is orientation. A reactive CX organization is one where the customer's urgency always sets the agenda. The team responds to what the customer surfaces. It solves the problems the customer names. It works on the timeline the customer imposes. This is not customer-centricity — it is customer-dependency. And the distinction matters enormously, because a team that is dependent on customers to name its priorities cannot build the trust, the foresight, or the strategic relationships that make a CX organization genuinely valuable.

The shift from reactive to proactive — from an organization that responds to one that connects — is the most consequential leadership transition in the CX function. It is also one of the most mismanaged, because most leaders try to make it by changing tactics when the real work is changing structure, signal flow, and the team's fundamental definition of its own job.

This guide is a 90-day transition framework. It is not a turnaround plan for a failing organization. It is a reorientation plan for a capable one that is pointed in the wrong direction.

Reactive organizations are not failing organizations. They are capable organizations that have been designed — usually by accident — to wait. The transition is not about working harder. It is about redesigning what you are waiting for.

The Diagnostic: Are You Reactive or Connected?

Before a 90-day plan, an honest 20-minute diagnostic. The questions below are not rhetorical. They have observable answers in your organization, and the gap between the reactive column and the connected column in each row is a direct measure of how much transition work lies ahead.

Reactive vs. Connected — Organizational Diagnostic
Question Reactive Organization Risk Connected Organization Target
How does your team learn that a customer is at risk? The customer tells us — through an escalation, a complaint, a renewal objection, or silence. We identify the signal 4–8 weeks before the customer expresses it, and we act on it before the conversation becomes difficult.
What sets the agenda for the CSM's week? Incoming requests, escalations, and whatever came up in the last customer interaction. A prioritized view of which accounts need attention this week, generated from health score data and engagement signals.
How does your PS team know a project is going off track? The PM tells us in a status call, or the customer escalates, or a milestone is missed. A project risk score surfaces the trajectory before the milestone is missed, triggering a delivery review before the customer is affected.
What is the primary topic of your weekly leadership team meeting? Reviewing what happened last week — escalations handled, tickets closed, projects delivered. Reviewing what is likely to happen next week — accounts to watch, risk trends, proactive plays in motion.
When do you typically learn about a customer's renewal risk? At or after the renewal conversation, when the customer raises concerns or requests a discount. 90+ days before renewal, based on health trajectory, adoption data, and executive sponsor engagement patterns.
How does cross-functional coordination happen? When a situation escalates enough to require it — usually reactively and under pressure. Through regular structured reviews of shared signals, with pre-defined escalation paths that activate automatically at thresholds.

If the reactive column describes your organization more accurately than the connected column in three or more rows, you have your transition mandate. The 90-day plan that follows is designed for exactly that starting point.

The 90-Day Transition Plan

The plan is organized into three 30-day phases. Each phase has a primary objective, a set of concrete actions, and a completion test — a specific observable condition that tells you the phase has been accomplished rather than merely performed. Do not move to the next phase until the completion test for the current one has been met.

D
1–30
Days 1 – 30
See Clearly
Build the signal infrastructure your team doesn't currently have

The first 30 days are not about changing anything your team does. They are about changing what your team can see. You cannot lead a proactive organization if you are operating on the same information that produced the reactive one. Before you introduce new playbooks, new processes, or new expectations, you need a clear, honest picture of where every account in your portfolio actually stands — not where your CSMs believe it stands based on relationship feel, but where the data says it stands.

This means auditing your current data sources, identifying the gaps, and establishing a minimum viable health signal for every customer-facing function. For Customer Success, that is a health score with at least three data inputs. For Professional Services, it is a project risk score with at least four. For Support, it is a sentiment trend and an escalation precursor flag. You do not need perfect data. You need consistent data that is better than what your team currently has — which in most reactive organizations is anecdote and intuition.

In parallel, run a portfolio audit. Categorize every account in your CS portfolio into four buckets: thriving, stable, stalling, and at-risk. Do this with your health data, not with your CSMs' opinions. The gap between what the data shows and what the relationship feels like is the most important discovery of Phase 1. It will tell you where your team's attention has been misallocated, and it will give you the first concrete input for Phase 2.

Phase 1 Actions
Audit all data sources across CS, PS, Support, and Education for completeness and consistency
Establish minimum viable health signal for every customer-facing function
Run a full portfolio audit — categorize every account by health data, not relationship feel
Document the gap between data-informed health and CSM-perceived health for every at-risk account
Establish a weekly signal review meeting — not a status report meeting, a signal discussion
Identify the three data gaps most likely to produce blind spots in the next 90 days and assign owners
Phase 1 Completion Test

You can walk into any leadership meeting and answer the question "which of our accounts are at risk right now?" with data, not with a list of escalations. You have a documented portfolio segmentation that was produced by signal, not by sentiment.

D
31–60
Days 31 – 60
Act Deliberately
Replace reactive workflows with signal-triggered playbooks

With a clear view of your portfolio established in Phase 1, Phase 2 is about changing how your team acts on what it sees. The reactive organization acts when the customer pushes. The connected organization acts when the signal fires — before the customer has to. Phase 2 builds the playbook infrastructure that makes that possible.

Start with the accounts your Phase 1 audit identified as stalling or at-risk. For each one, define a specific intervention: what action, by whom, by when, and what the expected outcome is. These are your first proactive plays. Run them manually. Do not automate yet — the goal is to build the habit of signal-triggered action before you build the machinery that scales it. When your team sees a proactive intervention work — when a CSM reaches out based on a health score alert and the customer says "I was just about to call you" — the cultural shift begins.

Simultaneously, redesign your weekly leadership rhythm. The reactive organization's weekly meeting reviews what happened. The connected organization's weekly meeting reviews what is about to happen. This single change — from retrospective to prospective — is one of the highest-leverage leadership actions in the transition. It signals to every function leader in the room what you are now optimizing for, and it gradually reshapes what they prepare and present.

Phase 2 Actions
Build intervention playbooks for each health stage — at-risk, stalling, stable, thriving
Run manual proactive plays on every at-risk account from the Phase 1 audit
Redesign the weekly leadership meeting format from retrospective to prospective
Define and publish intervention SLAs — how quickly must a signal be acted on by each function
Track proactive engagement rate as a team metric for the first time
Celebrate and name the first proactive saves — publicly, in the team meeting
Phase 2 Completion Test

Your team has run at least five proactive interventions that were triggered by a signal rather than a customer request. At least one of those interventions has produced a documented positive outcome. Your weekly leadership meeting is spending more time on what is coming than on what already happened.

D
61–90
Days 61 – 90
Connect Structurally
Build the cross-functional accountability that makes proactivity sustainable

Phases 1 and 2 improve what your CX team can see and do within its own functions. Phase 3 addresses the structural reality that the customer's experience does not respect functional boundaries — and that proactive CX requires connection across PS, Support, Education, Managed Services, and CS in ways that most organizational designs actively resist.

The work of Phase 3 is designing the cross-functional connective tissue: shared signals, shared reviews, shared accountability metrics, and shared handoff standards that ensure a customer does not fall into the gap between functions. This is the work that most leaders know needs doing and most consistently defer, because it requires navigating the organizational complexity of multiple functions, multiple P&Ls, and multiple leaders who have been optimizing their own lanes since before you started this transition.

Do not try to solve all of it at once. Focus Phase 3 on the two or three handoff points in your organization that produce the most customer friction — the PS-to-CS handoff, the Support-to-CS escalation path, the Education-to-adoption connection. Design a shared signal and a shared review for each one. Get agreement on what a successful handoff looks like and what the receiving function's responsibility is when it is not. Build this into your operating rhythm, not just your policy documents.

Phase 3 Actions
Map every cross-functional handoff and rate it by friction level and customer impact
Design shared signals and joint review cadences for the two highest-friction handoffs
Establish a cross-functional NRR metric that every function leader is accountable to, not just CS
Define handoff standards with explicit receiving-function responsibilities and SLAs
Run a 90-day retrospective — compare alert-to-action latency, proactive engagement rate, and at-risk account count to Day 1 baseline
Present the transition story to leadership — what changed, what the data shows, and what the next 90 days are designed to accomplish
Phase 3 Completion Test

At least two cross-functional handoffs have documented shared standards and are being reviewed jointly on a recurring cadence. Your proactive engagement rate is measurably higher than it was on Day 1. Your team can articulate, without being prompted, the difference between what they were doing 90 days ago and what they are doing now.

What Day 91 Looks Like

On Day 91, you will not have completed the transition. The shift from reactive to connected is not a 90-day project — it is a multi-year organizational development effort. What 90 days produces is a different starting point: a team that has experienced what proactive work feels like, a signal infrastructure that is better than the one you inherited, a leadership rhythm that is oriented toward the future rather than the past, and two or three cross-functional connections that are structurally sound rather than personality-dependent.

That is enough to build from. It is also enough to demonstrate to your leadership team, your customers, and your own organization that the direction has changed. Culture shifts happen incrementally — in the small moments when a CSM catches a risk before it escalates, when a PS lead flags a delivery concern before the customer asks about it, when a support engineer recognizes a pattern rather than just resolving a ticket. The 90-day plan is not designed to produce those moments directly. It is designed to produce the conditions under which those moments become possible — and then, gradually, normal.

CXKonnect Principle

The transition from reactive to connected is not a process improvement. It is a leadership transformation. The process changes are real and necessary — but they are downstream of a more fundamental shift in what you, as a leader, pay attention to, ask about, and hold people accountable for every day. The 90-day plan gives you the structure. Your own behavior gives it the meaning.

The Most Common Reason This Fails

The 90-day transition plan has a single most common failure mode, and it is not a data problem or a technology problem or a process problem. It is a leadership attention problem. Somewhere in the middle of Phase 2 — usually around Day 45 — a significant escalation arrives. A major customer is at risk. An implementation has gone sideways. An executive is calling for a response. And the leader who was building a proactive organization drops everything to manage the crisis reactively, signals to the team that crisis response is still the real priority, and watches the transition quietly decelerate.

This is not a failure of commitment. It is a failure of design. The transition plan must account for the fact that reactive situations will not pause while you build a proactive organization. The answer is not to ignore the crises — it is to handle them without abandoning the transition work, and to use them explicitly as teaching moments: "Here is the situation we are managing today. Here is the signal that we should have caught three weeks ago. Here is the data point we are adding to our signal model because of what we learned."

The reactive crisis, handled in this way, becomes evidence for the transition rather than a setback from it. The team sees that the leader is not just building a better process — they are building a different organization. And that recognition, repeated enough times, is the culture shift.

A Final Word to the Leader Beginning This Work

Your teams are not underperforming. They are unconnected — to each other, to the signals their customers are sending, and to a shared definition of what success looks like that goes beyond surviving the current week. The capabilities you need are almost certainly already in your organization. The work of the next 90 days is not to build new capabilities from scratch. It is to connect the ones you have — to each other, to a clear signal of what the customer actually needs, and to a leadership model that makes proactivity not just possible, but expected.

Begin with Day 1. The diagnostic will tell you where to start. The rest of the plan will tell you what to do when you get there. And the team you build will tell you, somewhere around Day 80, that they finally feel like they are ahead of it — and that feeling is worth every difficult conversation the next 90 days will require.
